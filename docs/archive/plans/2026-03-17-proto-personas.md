# Skygest — Proto-Personas

Three proto-personas covering the primary audience segments. All assumptions — validate through user research.

## Persona 1: Newsletter Nadia (Primary)

**Segment:** Informed energy-curious reader

**Bio:** 34, works in climate-adjacent policy at a state agency. Not an energy engineer but deeply informed. Subscribes to 4 energy newsletters (Carbon Brief, Heatmap, Bloomberg Green, Volts), listens to Volts and The Carbon Copy on her commute. Follows ~30 energy experts on Bluesky. Reads policy documents when they drop, not just the coverage.

**Quotes:**
- "I saw the headline about BC hydro imports but I want to see what Blake Shaffer actually said about it, not what Reuters wrote about what he said"
- "By the time a newsletter covers something, I've usually already seen the thread — but I miss threads all the time"
- "I wish I could see what the experts are citing, not just what they're concluding"

**Pains:**
- Scrolls Bluesky for 30-40 min/day and still misses important threads
- Newsletters arrive on someone else's schedule and filter through someone else's judgment
- Charts posted by experts are fascinating but she can't trace them to the underlying data
- Great threads disappear — she bookmarks some but loses track

**Goals:**
- Never miss an important expert thread on her topics (hydro, grid, electrification)
- Go deeper than the news cycle — understand the expert discourse that drives the headlines
- Build her own understanding over time, not just consume takes

**Attitudes & Influences:**
- Decision-making: Individual consumer — would pay for a newsletter-quality product ($5-15/mo)
- Influenced by: expert threads she sees shared, podcast recommendations, peer forwarding
- Beliefs: primary sources > journalism > hot takes. Skeptical of tools that oversimplify.

## Persona 2: Expert Eli (Creator)

**Segment:** Energy domain expert who creates analysis on Bluesky

**Bio:** 47, energy economist at a Canadian university. Posts data-rich threads on Bluesky 2-3 times a week. Has 4,200 followers. Creates his own charts from public data (StatCan, provincial hydro reports, EIA). Occasionally gets quoted by journalists. Blake Shaffer is the archetype.

**Quotes:**
- "I spend 3 hours making a thread with 10 charts and it gets 20 likes and disappears in a day — meanwhile some hot take with no data gets 500"
- "Journalists DM me asking for my old threads and I have to scroll back through my own timeline to find them"
- "I'd love to know when another expert responds to my analysis with real substance, not just 'great thread'"

**Pains:**
- High-effort analysis threads get low visibility relative to their quality
- No persistent home for his work — threads are ephemeral by design
- Can't easily see when domain experts (not random followers) engage with his analysis
- Journalists and analysts find his work useful but discovery is accidental

**Goals:**
- Have his analysis reach the people who would actually use it (analysts, policymakers, journalists)
- See substantive expert engagement with his work, not just like counts
- Build a body of work that's discoverable over time, not buried in a timeline

**Attitudes & Influences:**
- Decision-making: Not a buyer — benefits from amplification. Would advocate for a product that presents his work well.
- Influenced by: peer recognition from other domain experts, citation by journalists, policy impact
- Beliefs: data > opinion. Frustrated by platforms that reward engagement over substance.

## Persona 3: Journalist Jade (Deadline-driven)

**Segment:** Energy journalist at an online publication

**Bio:** 29, covers energy transition for an online publication (Heatmap, Canary Media, or similar). Writes 3-4 pieces a week on tight deadlines. Uses Bluesky and Twitter as primary source-finding tools. Needs to find the expert who said the smart thing about today's news, fast.

**Quotes:**
- "My editor asks 'what are experts saying about the Hormuz situation' and I need the answer in 20 minutes, not 2 hours"
- "I know the good threads exist because I see people referencing them, but I can't always find the original"
- "The charts experts post are often better than anything I can source from a press release — if only I could trace where the data came from"

**Pains:**
- Finding expert threads on a breaking story is manual and unreliable — search is terrible on Bluesky
- Can't quickly identify which experts have commented on a specific topic today
- Charts are gold for articles but she can't cite "screenshot from Bluesky" — needs the underlying source
- Deadline pressure means she defaults to the same 5 experts she already knows, missing others

**Goals:**
- Find the expert thread with the data before her competitors do
- Trace chart data back to citable primary sources
- Discover experts she doesn't already follow who are saying important things on her beat

**Attitudes & Influences:**
- Decision-making: Could expense a tool subscription. Publication might pay for a team license.
- Influenced by: what other journalists are citing, expert recommendations, speed of access
- Beliefs: first to the story matters. Primary sources > press releases. Experts on Bluesky are often ahead of formal publications.

## How Personas Map to Product

| Feature | Nadia (Reader) | Eli (Expert) | Jade (Journalist) |
|---------|---------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Signal-ranked feed | Primary use | Sees his work surfaced | Scans for story leads |
| Thread-as-document | Reads deeply | His threads presented well | Skims for key data |
| Chart vision + alt text | Understands charts better | His charts become accessible | Can trace data for citation |
| Source attribution | Follows links to learn | His sources get credited | Cites the underlying data |
| Temporal linking | Tracks narratives over time | Body of work persists | Finds prior expert analysis |
| Discussion preservation | Reads expert replies | Sees substantive engagement | Finds additional sources |

## Validation Plan

All personas are assumption-based. Key hypotheses to validate:
- [ASSUMPTION] Nadia exists in sufficient numbers — are there enough informed-but-not-professional energy readers?
- [ASSUMPTION] Eli would actively engage with a product that surfaces his work — or would he see it as content extraction?
- [ASSUMPTION] Jade's publication would pay for a tool — or is "free Bluesky search" good enough?
- [ASSUMPTION] All three segments want the same core product — or do they need fundamentally different experiences?
